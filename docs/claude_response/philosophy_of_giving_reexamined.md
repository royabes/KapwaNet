# The Philosophy of "Giving" - Reexamined

*Why "giving" may be as problematic as "asking"*

---

## The Problem with "Giving"

When we say someone is "giving," we imply:

1. **Ownership** - They possess something
2. **Surplus** - They have more than they need
3. **Transfer** - Something moves from them to another
4. **Loss** - After giving, they have less
5. **Hierarchy** - The giver is in the position of power

This framing is deeply embedded in Western property thinking. But is it true?

---

## Part I: What Is Actually Happening?

### The Illusion of Ownership

Consider: A person "gives" food to a neighbor.

But where did that food come from?
- Grown by farmers they never met
- From seeds saved by generations before
- In soil made by billions of organisms over millennia
- Transported by workers, roads built by others
- Purchased with money earned through community participation

**The person never "owned" that food in any absolute sense.** They were a temporary steward, a node through which the food passed.

### The Illusion of Giving

When the food moves to the neighbor, what actually happened?

Option A (Western view): Person A **gave** food to Person B.

Option B (Flow view): Food that was flowing through Person A continued flowing through Person B.

In Option B, there is no "giving" - only flow. The food was always moving. Person A simply didn't stop it.

### The Illusion of Loss

"After giving, they have less."

But do they?

What did Person A receive?
- Connection with their neighbor
- Participation in community life
- The experience of being a channel for flow
- Meaning, purpose
- Perhaps future reciprocity
- The absence of guilt from hoarding while others go without

**The "giver" is also receiving.** The transaction is not one-way.

## Part II: Historical and Philosophical Perspectives

### Marcel Mauss: The Gift (1925)

Mauss studied gift economies and found that gifts are never "free":

> "To give is to show one's superiority... To accept without returning or repaying more is to face subordination."

In gift economies, the gift creates **obligation**. The receiver is bound to the giver. This is still hierarchy, just hidden.

**Insight**: Even in "gift" cultures, the power dynamics remain unless we fundamentally rethink what's happening.

### Lewis Hyde: The Gift (1983)

Hyde distinguishes between **commodity exchange** and **gift exchange**:

- Commodity: Objects exchange for equivalent value. Relationship ends.
- Gift: Something extra passes. Relationship deepens.

But more radically, Hyde notes:

> "A gift that cannot move loses its gift properties."

The gift isn't an object - it's the **movement itself**. When you stop the gift, it dies. When you pass it on, it lives.

**Insight**: You don't "give" a gift. You participate in its movement.

### Derrida: Given Time (1992)

Derrida argues that a true gift is **impossible**:

- If the receiver knows they received a gift, they feel gratitude/obligation - the gift becomes exchange
- If the giver knows they gave a gift, they feel satisfaction - the gift becomes self-reward
- For a gift to be truly "given," neither party can recognize it as such

**Insight**: The moment we call something "giving," we've already corrupted it.

### The Potlatch

In Pacific Northwest Indigenous cultures, the potlatch was a ceremony of **giving away wealth**. Status came not from accumulation but from **how much you could give away**.

But even this contained hierarchy - the greatest giver had the highest status.

**Insight**: Even inverted, the giving/receiving binary creates hierarchy.

### Buddhist Dana (Generosity)

In Buddhism, *dana* (generosity) is practiced to **let go of attachment**. The point is not to help the receiver but to **liberate the giver** from clinging.

The Buddha said there are three types of giving:
1. **Beggarly giving** - giving leftovers, what you don't need
2. **Friendly giving** - giving what you yourself would use
3. **Kingly giving** - giving the best, before yourself

But even higher than these: **giving without a giver, receiver, or gift** - the realization that all three are empty.

**Insight**: The highest form of "giving" is recognizing there is no giver.

### Ubuntu: "I Am Because We Are"

In Ubuntu philosophy, the question "who gave?" makes less sense. If we are fundamentally interconnected, then resources moving between us aren't "given" - they're simply moving within one body.

When your left hand passes food to your right hand, do we say the left hand "gave" to the right hand?

**Insight**: In true community, there is no giving - only circulation within one organism.

## Part III: The Deeper Truth

### What If No One Gives?

What if we reframe everything as **flow** rather than giving/receiving?

| Giving Paradigm | Flow Paradigm |
|-----------------|---------------|
| "I gave you food" | "Food flowed through me to you" |
| "She's so generous" | "She's a wide channel" |
| "He gave his time" | "Time flowed through his participation" |
| "They donated money" | "Resources moved through them" |
| "I need to give back" | "The flow continues" |

In the flow paradigm:
- No one is superior (wide channel â‰  better person)
- No one loses (the channel isn't diminished by flow)
- No obligation is created (water doesn't owe the river)
- No ego accumulates (the channel doesn't get credit)

### The Channel vs. The Source

**Giving paradigm**: I am a source. I have, therefore I can give.

**Flow paradigm**: I am a channel. Things pass through me. I don't create them, and they don't belong to me.

In the flow paradigm:
- Hoarding = blocking the channel (unnatural)
- Flowing = being what you are (natural)
- "Generosity" = simply not obstructing

### The Problem of "Generosity"

We praise people for being "generous." But what does that mean?

In the giving paradigm: They gave more than required. They sacrificed.

In the flow paradigm: They didn't block what was meant to flow. They were simply healthy channels.

We don't praise the river for being "generous" when it flows. It's just doing what rivers do.

**What if humans are also meant to flow?** Then "generosity" isn't virtue - it's just not being blocked.

And "stinginess" isn't vice - it's just blockage, sickness, obstruction. Something to heal, not condemn.

## Part V: The Unified Paradigm

### Dissolving Both Binaries

We've now challenged:
1. **Asking/Receiving** - Reframed as "opening space for participation"
2. **Giving** - Reframed as "being a channel for flow"

What remains is simpler:

**There is only participation in flow.**

- Sometimes the flow is toward you
- Sometimes the flow is through you
- Sometimes you open space
- Sometimes you fill space
- There is no giver, no receiver
- There is only the web, circulating

### The Core Realization

> We are not givers and receivers.
> We are not even channels.
> We are the flow itself, temporarily shaped into forms.

When resources move between us, it's not transfer of property between separate beings. It's **the universe moving within itself**.

This sounds mystical, but it's also practical:

- The food that enters you literally becomes you
- The care you receive shapes who you become
- The time someone spends with you becomes your memory
- Nothing is truly "given" because nothing is truly separate

## Conclusion: Beyond Giving

The problem with "giving":
- Implies ownership
- Creates hierarchy
- Expects recognition
- Builds ego

The alternative isn't "receiving" - that's the same paradigm inverted.

The alternative is **flow**:
- No owner
- No hierarchy
- No transaction
- Just movement

In a healthy community, things move. That's all.

KapwaNet's job: **Remove the obstructions.**

Not to encourage giving (that still implies someone should give).
Not to make receiving easier (that still implies someone is receiving).

Just to **let things flow** that are already trying to flow.
